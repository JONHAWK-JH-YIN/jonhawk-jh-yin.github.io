About Me

I am an undergraduate student in Economics at the University of Macau, with academic training in economics, political science, and quantitative methods. In Fall 2026, I will study as an exchange student at The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen. My core research interests lie in American legislative politics and electoral politics, with a particular focus on how legislative institutions, political actors, committee systems, electoral incentives, and policymaking processes shape representation, institutional performance, and governance outcomes. More broadly, I am interested in political institutions, public administration, comparative political economy, and governance issues in East Asia and the Greater China region.

My research focuses on the internal dynamics of legislative institutions, including agenda-setting, committee organization, legislative behavior, institutional design, interest representation, and policy outputs. Methodologically, I am interested in causal inference, quantitative text analysis, social network analysis, statistical modeling, and computational social science approaches to the study of political institutions.

I am also interested in AI for Research and structured data engineering, particularly their applications in political economy and historical social science. My work involves using large language models, prompt engineering, Python, R, regular expressions, and machine learning tools to extract, clean, structure, and analyze data from historical archives, local gazetteers, political texts, and administrative documents.

My current and past research experiences cover American legislative politics, economic history and AI-assisted data engineering, regional collaborative governance and social network analysis, and political text analysis. Across these projects, I have contributed to literature reviews, variable design, historical material coding, dataset construction, coding workflows, prompt design, text processing, and empirical analysis.

I am also interested in teaching and knowledge communication. I previously taught an R Programming and Data Science course at a residential college at the University of Macau, where I helped design the course outline, prepare teaching materials, and develop R Quarto-based lecture slides.

Looking ahead, I plan to pursue a PhD in political science, with American legislative politics, electoral politics, and political institutions as my main research directions. Before entering doctoral study, I hope to seek a full-time research assistant position where I can further strengthen my methodological training, deepen my understanding of legislative and institutional research, and contribute to rigorous empirical political science projects.
